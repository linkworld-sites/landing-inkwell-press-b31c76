Your wedding invitation is the first impression your celebration makes on every guest who receives it. Before a single syllable is spoken, before anyone sets foot in the venue, the invitation arrives in their hands — and it tells them, in every tactile and visual detail, what kind of day awaits. For couples who want that first impression to feel genuinely considered, **letterpress wedding invitations** remain the gold standard.

This guide covers everything you need to know to choose the right suite, work with the right studio, and avoid the most common missteps.

---

## What makes letterpress different

Letterpress is a relief-printing process. Type and imagery set into a form are inked and pressed into the paper with measurable physical force. The result is a debossed impression — a shallow valley pressed into the sheet — that you can see and feel simultaneously.

Digital printing, offset lithography, and even thermography produce flat ink on a flat surface. Letterpress produces *texture*. The paper itself becomes part of the design.

For **luxury wedding invitations**, this distinction matters enormously. The impression communicates craft and intentionality in a way that no ink-on-paper technique can replicate. Guests who have never heard the word "letterpress" will still pick up the invitation and say: *this feels different.*

## Paper stock: the most important decision you will make

Cotton paper is the standard for **letterpress wedding invitations** because it accepts the impression without tearing and holds it without rebounding. Wood-pulp papers compress and spring back. Cotton fibres hold.

The two papers we recommend most often:

- **Crane Lettra 110 lb** — A classic for single-sheet invitations. Soft, receptive, and available in both Fluorescent White and Pearl White. The 110 lb weight has enough presence without feeling heavy.
- **Crane Lettra 220 lb (double-thick)** — For suites where the invitation card itself should feel substantial, two sheets of 110 lb are cold-pressed together. The result is a 5 mm card that sits in the hand like a small book. This is our most frequently requested paper for bespoke wedding work.

Avoid coated stocks. The coating fills the paper's tooth and resists the impression, producing shallower debossing and often uneven ink distribution.

## Ink and colour: less is more

Letterpress ink is oil-based and mixed by hand to a specific viscosity for each run. A single colour prints per pass; two colours require two passes, two set-ups, and careful registration.

For most **custom letterpress stationery**, we recommend starting with one or two colours maximum:

- **One colour** — Typically the most elegant result. Navy, forest green, deep burgundy, and warm black all print beautifully. The impression itself adds a second visual element without requiring a second ink.
- **Two colours** — Works well when one colour carries the type and a second appears as a smaller accent: a monogram, a floral detail, a return address in a lighter tone.
- **Foil and letterpress combined** — Hot foil stamping can be run in conjunction with letterpress passes to add metallic elements. This adds both cost and complexity; reserve it for a single focal point rather than applying it throughout.

Pantone matching is standard. Bring reference swatches or provide a Pantone code if you have a specific colour in mind. Screens and monitors are not reliable colour references for print.

## The suite: what to include and what to skip

A traditional **letterpress wedding invitation** suite comprises:

1. **The invitation** — The centrepiece. Date, time, venue, and hosts.
2. **The details card** — Accommodation recommendations, directions, travel notes, dress code.
3. **The RSVP card and envelope** — Pre-addressed to the couple. If you are collecting RSVPs digitally, a response card is optional but often retained for the tactile completeness of the suite.
4. **The outer envelope** — Addressed by hand or by a calligrapher. The envelope is the first thing guests touch; it deserves the same care as the contents.
5. **The inner envelope** — Traditional, but increasingly optional. Useful if you are managing household names carefully (listing each guest by name rather than simply addressing to the household).

Optional additions include venue maps (illustrated line drawings print beautifully in letterpress), menu cards, ceremony programmes, and escort cards for the reception.

Our consistent advice: *decide what is necessary, then add nothing*. A spare suite with a single ink and exceptional paper will outlast a busy suite with four typefaces and gold foil.

## Timeline: plan earlier than you think

**Custom letterpress stationery** takes time. The stages are:

| Stage | Typical duration |
|---|---|
| Initial design and briefing | 1–2 weeks |
| Design proofs and revisions | 2–4 weeks |
| Final proof approval | 1 week |
| Press set-up and printing | 2–3 weeks |
| Finishing (cutting, packing) | 1 week |
| **Total from first contact** | **7–11 weeks** |

This means that for a wedding in late summer, you should be in conversation with your studio no later than April. For winter weddings, September is the safe starting point.

Save-the-dates, if you are sending them, add a separate production cycle before the main suite.

## What to ask a letterpress studio

Before you commission, ask:

- **Do you print in-house or outsource?** In-house means the studio has direct control over quality at every stage. Outsourced printing is not necessarily inferior, but you should know.
- **Can I see paper samples?** A reputable studio will send swatches. You cannot judge cotton paper from a photograph.
- **What is your proofing process?** You should see a physical press proof — not just a digital PDF — before the full run begins.
- **How do you handle guest addressing?** Will they print on envelopes, work with a calligrapher, or is this left to you?
- **What is your minimum order quantity?** Letterpress economics favour larger runs; minimum quantities vary by studio.

## Budget: an honest overview

**Luxury wedding invitations** produced by letterpress carry a premium that reflects hand production, specialist materials, and long press times. A fully bespoke suite — invitation, details card, RSVP set, and envelopes — for 100 guests typically ranges from £1,800 to £3,500 depending on the number of ink colours, paper weight, and finishing choices.

That per-unit cost is higher than digital printing by a meaningful margin. The difference is tactile permanence. Your guests will not discard a letterpress invitation the week after the wedding. They will keep it.
